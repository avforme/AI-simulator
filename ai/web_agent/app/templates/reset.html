Each episode starts with the same scenario. How the episode evolves will depend on market returns and your investment decisions.

The scenario

It is the year 2020. The client is a 65 year old female. They are, and will remain, in good health, with a life expectancy 3 years long than the average for a 65 year old female. They start with $500,000 of tax free investment assets, and Social Security providing $16,000 per year of inflation indexed guaranteed income,

Annual well-being varies depending upon the annual consumption amount. Technically, well-being is described as exhibiting constant relative risk aversion with a coefficient of relative risk aversion of 3, as shown in the graph to the right.

The client has an unlimited psychological ability to stomach changes in investment portfolio value. They have no bequest motive. They simply seek to maximize their overall well-being while they are alive.

Your task

You have to decide how much the client should consume each period, and how they should invest the remainder. The available asset classes are:

  • Stocks - stocks are expected to return 1.5% below their historical value and have a geometric mean return after inflation of 3.6% with an annual volatility of 17.4%.
  • Government bonds - you pick the duration; bond returns will be similar to average historical values; 5 year duration bonds have an average 1.3% return after inflation and a 5.7% volatility.
  • Inflation-indexed government bonds (TIPS) - you pick the duration; TIPS will have similar to average post-2004 historical values; 5 year duration TIPS have an average 1.0% return and 3.8% volatility; the spread between government bonds and TIPS will match inflation expectations.

So you have full information on the investment scenario you will be presented with the current yield curve. It isn't critical to use it, but small gains may be possible through market timing. Both short-term interest rates and inflation exhibit larger standard deviations than is normal. This is an unwelcome side effect of the simple bond pricing model used, and can be ignored. Values for longer durations are more realistic.

Inflation will largely match historical values. Occasionally the inflation rate may may become negative. This too is an unwanted side effect of the bond pricing model that can be ignored. It should not be a cause for alarm.

Single Premium Immediate Annuities (SPIAs) may be purchased at any age in any amount, and there is no transactional overhead from doing so. Non-inflation indexed SPIAs have a money's worth ratio of 100% after fees when measured against government bonds. Inflation indexed SPIAs have a money's worth ratio of 94% when measured against TIPS.

The client will likely die at some point during the episode, however each episode lasts until age 120. I wish to understand the strategy you would use if the client were still alive. Once the client's wealth has been spent down you can indicate that the client should consume the full amount of their income for each remaining time step to avoid having to step all the way through until age 120.

This is a simplified world. There are no taxes or transaction costs to consider. There is no need for an emergency fund.

You should use your professional judgment, and if you wish a calculator, but no other software. You may take as long as you wish to make your decisions. You should complete a whole number of episodes, rather than stopping midway through an episode.

All amounts are reported in and entered in year 2020 inflation-adjusted dollars.
